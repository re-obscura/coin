What is sterling silver?

Sterling silver is an alloy that contains 92.5% silver (925 proof) and up to 7.5% other metals, most commonly copper.

Pure 999 silver is too soft to create large pieces, so it is usually combined with copper. This preserves the strength, flexibility and attractiveness of the noble metal. To improve the quality of the alloy, other metals such as germanium, zinc, platinum or titanium are sometimes used, but copper-added alloy remains the standard.

"Sterling silver and regular silver are the same thing. The question about their difference does not make sense, because sterling is ordinary silver. It is this material that is used to create jewelry."

* The highest grade of silver is 999, but it is not used to make jewelry and cutlery. Pure silver is used in the medical and space industries and is only available as bank bullion.

How do we grade silver?

Grading silver involves several key steps to ensure the precious metals purity, quality, and value are accurately assessed.

1

Physical Inspection

Verifying color and luster. Pure silver has a bright white appearance with a high degree of reflectiveness.

2

Hallmark Tests

Examining stamps like 925 (sterling). These are often located in inconspicuous areas like the underside of flatware.

3

Chemical Tests

Acid testing where a reaction indicates purity. Real silver typically shows a creamy white reaction.

4

X-Ray Technology

Using X-ray fluorescence (XRF) for non-destructive, detailed analysis of the metals elemental composition.

5

Craftsmanship

Assessing design intricacy and workmanship. Handcrafted pieces by renowned artists hold more value.

6

Provenance

Items with well-documented histories or significant previous ownership can fetch higher values.

"In summary, grading silver is a multifaceted process that goes beyond the visual examination. It incorporates hallmark verification, chemical and technological testing, craftsmanship assessment, and provenance research to determine the true quality and value of silver artifacts."
